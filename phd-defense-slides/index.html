Part I
Polar actions on symmetric spaces

Polar actions

$M$ complete Riemannian manifold, $\s{H}\curvearrowright M$ proper isometric action.

$\s{H}\curvearrowright M$ is polar if $\exists \, \Sigma \subseteq M$ with:

  • $\Sigma \cap (\s{H}\cdot p)\neq \varnothing$ for all $p\in M$,
  • $T_p \Sigma \perp T_p (\s{H}\cdot p)$ for all $p\in \Sigma$.

$\Sigma$ is a section.

Examples on $\RR\s{H}^2=\s{SL}(2,\RR)/\s{SO}(2)$

Examples from algebra

  • $M=\s{Sym}_0(n,\RR), \; \s{H}=\s{SO}(n)$ acting by conjugation.
    • $\Sigma=\{ \textup{diagonal matrices} \}$ (spectral theorem).
  • $M=\s{H}=\s{G}$ compact Lie group, $g\cdot x = gxg^{-1}$.
    • $\Sigma = \s{T}^r$ maximal torus of $\s{G}$ (maximal torus theorem).
Two actions $\s{H}_1 \curvearrowright M,\, \s{H}_2 \curvearrowright M$ are orbit equivalent if
$\exists \, f\in I(M)$ with $f(\s{H}_1 \cdot p)=\s{H}_2 \cdot f(p)$ for all $p\in M$.
Main problem. Given $M$, classify polar actions on $M$
up to orbit equivalence.

Symmetric spaces

$M$ is a symmetric space if every $p\in M$ admits a geodesic reflection $s_p$.
  • $M=\s{G}/\s{K}$, where $\s{G}=I^0(M)$ and $\s{K}=\s{G}_o$, $o\in M$.
  • $\Theta \colon g\in \s{G}\mapsto s_o g s_o$ group involution $\rightsquigarrow$ $\theta=\Theta_*$ involution of $\g{g}$.
  • $\g{g}=\g{k}\oplus \g{p}$ Cartan decomposition ($\mathbb{Z}_2$-grading).
  • $\g{p}\cong \g{g}/\g{k}\cong T_o M$.
  • Rank of $M$ $=$ dimension of any maximal flat in $M$ $=$ dimension of any maximal abelian subspace $\g{a}\subseteq \g{p}$.

Symmetric spaces of noncompact type

  • $M=\s{G}/\s{K}$ (irreducible) symmetric space of noncompact type.
  • $\theta\in \Aut(\g{g})$ Cartan involution, $\g{g}=\g{k}\oplus\g{p}$ Cartan decomposition.
  • $\c{B}\vert_{\g{p}\times\g{p}}$ is the Riemannian metric on $\g{p}\cong T_o M$ (up to scaling).
  • $\left< X,Y \right> = -\c{B}(\theta X,Y)$ inner product on $\g{g}$.

Root space decomposition

  • $\g{a}\subseteq \g{p}$ maximal abelian subspace.
  • For $\lambda \in \g{a}^*$, define $$\g{g}_\lambda = \{ X\in \g{g} \colon [H,X]=\lambda(H)X \text{ for all } H\in \g{a} \}.$$
  • $\Sigma \subseteq \g{a}^*$ set of roots. We obtain the root space decomposition

    • $$\g{g} = \g{g}_0 \oplus \bigoplus_{\lambda \in \Sigma} \g{g}_\lambda.$$

Iwasawa decomposition

  • Notion of positivity
    $\rightsquigarrow$ positive roots $\Sigma^+\subseteq \Sigma$.
  • Simple roots $\Lambda \subseteq \Sigma^+$.
  • $\Sigma^+$ induces a nilpotent subalgebra $$\g{n}=\bigoplus_{\lambda \in \Sigma^+}\g{g}_\lambda.$$

$B_2$

$\g{g}=\g{k}\oplus \g{a}\oplus \g{n}, \qquad \s{G}=\s{KAN}$.

The canonical extension method

  • $\Phi\subseteq \Lambda$ subset of simple roots.
  • Define $$\g{a}_\Phi = \bigcap_{\alpha \in \Phi} \ker \alpha, \qquad \g{n}_\Phi = \bigoplus_{\lambda \in \Sigma^+\smallsetminus \Sigma^+_{\Phi}}\g{g}_{\lambda}.$$
  • $\g{a}_\Phi\oplus\g{n}_\Phi$ subalgebra, $\s{A}_\Phi\s{N}_\Phi$ induces a polar homogeneous foliation.
  • $B_\Phi$ section of $\s{A}_\Phi\s{N}_\Phi\curvearrowright M$ through $o$ $\rightsquigarrow$ $\operatorname{rank}(B_\Phi)=\lvert \Phi \rvert$.
  • $B_\Phi$: boundary component of $M$ associated with $\Phi$.

$\s{H}\curvearrowright B_\Phi$

$\s{H}\s{A}_{\Phi}\s{N}_\Phi \curvearrowright M$

Theorem A (Díaz-Ramos, LN). $M$ irreducible symmetric space of noncompact type, $\c{F}$ codimension two polar homogeneous foliation on $M$. Then one of the following holds:
  1. $\c{F}$ is hyperpolar (BDT).
  2. $\c{F}$ is orbit equivalent to the canonical extension of a polar homogeneous foliation on some $B_\Phi$, $\lvert \Phi \rvert = 1$.

Theorem (Díaz-Ramos, LN). Every codimension two polar homogeneous foliation on $\F \s{H}^n$ is congruent to either an $\c{F}_\xi$ or an $\c{F}_{\g{v}}$.
Theorem B (Díaz-Ramos, LN). $M$ irreducible symmetric space of noncompact type. Every C2 polar nonhyperpolar homogeneous foliation on $M$ is congruent to the orbit foliation induced by either:
  1. $\s{S}_\xi$, where $\g{s}_\xi = (\ker \alpha)\oplus (\g{n}\ominus \RR \xi), \; \alpha\in \Lambda, \, \xi\in \g{g}_\alpha$, or
  2. $\s{S}_\g{v}$, where $\g{s}_\g{v} = \g{a}\oplus (\g{n}\ominus \g{v}), \; \alpha\in \Lambda, \, \g{v}\subseteq \g{g}_\alpha$ abelian.

Sketch of proof

Suppose $\c{F}$ is a cohomogeneity 2 polar nonhyperpolar homogeneous foliation on $M$.

  1. $\c{F}$ is induced by a closed connected solvable $\s{S}\subseteq \s{G}$.
  2. We may assume $\g{s}\subseteq \g{t}\oplus \g{a}\oplus \g{n}, \;\g{t}\subseteq \g{g}_0\cap \g{k}$ abelian.
  3. Using an algebraic characterization, we get $\g{s}_{\g{a}\oplus \g{n}}$ is an $\g{s}_\xi$ or an $\g{s}_{\g{v}}$.
  4. If $\g{s}_{\g{a}\oplus \g{n}}=\g{s}_{\xi}$ (respectively, $\g{s}_{\g{v}}$), then $\s{S}$ has the same orbits as $\s{S}_{\xi}$ (respectively, $\s{S}_{\g{v}}$).

Totally geodesic submanifolds

  • $M$ real analytic Riemannian manifold, $f\colon \Sigma \to M$ isometric immersion.
  • $f$ is totally geodesic if:
$\gamma \colon I \to \Sigma$ geodesic $\Rightarrow$ $f\circ \gamma\colon I \to M$ geodesic.

$\RR^k\subseteq \RR^n$

$\s{S}^k\subseteq \s{S}^n$

Naturally reductive homogeneous spaces

  • $M=\s{G}/\s{K}$ Riemannian homogeneous space, $o=e\s{K}$.
  • $\g{g}$ admits a reductive ($\s{K}$-module) decomposition $\g{g}=\g{k}\oplus \g{p}$.
  • $M$ is naturally reductive if $\exp_o(tX)=\Exp(tX)\cdot o, \; \forall X\in \g{p}$.
  • Canonical connection $\nabla^c \colon \g{X}(M)\times \g{X}(M)\to \g{X}(M):$ $$ (\nabla^c_{X^*}Y^*)_o = -[X,Y]_{\g{p}}. $$
  • Difference tensor $D=\nabla - \nabla^c \in \operatorname{Hom}(\Lambda^2 \g{p},\g{p})$: $$ D_X Y = \frac{1}{2}[X,Y]_{\g{p}}. $$

Totally geodesic subspaces

  • $f\colon \Sigma \to M$ totally geodesic. Then $f$ is determined by any tangent space $f_{*x}(T_x \Sigma), \;x\in \Sigma$.
  • Given $V\subseteq T_p M$, when does $V$ generate a totally geodesic submanifold?
Theorem (Cartan '51, Hermann '59). $M$ real analytic, $V\subseteq T_p M$. The following are equivalent:
  1. $V$ is a totally geodesic subspace.
  2. $V$ is $\nabla^k R$-invariant for all $k\geq 0$.

Totally geodesic submanifolds of $\s{G}/\s{K}$

  • $M=\s{G}/\s{K}$ naturally reductive homogeneous space.
  • Totally geodesic submanifolds through $o$ $\Leftrightarrow$ totally geodesic subspaces of $\g{p}$.
Tojo's criterion. $M$ naturally reductive, $\g{v}\subseteq \g{p}$. The following are equivalent:
  1. $\g{v}$ is a totally geodesic subspace.
  2. The subspace $e^{D_X}\g{v}$ is $R$-invariant for all $X\in \g{v}$.

$D$-invariant totally geodesic submanifolds

  • $M=\s{G}/\s{K}$ naturally reductive, $\Sigma \to M$ submanifold.
  • $\Sigma$ is $D$-invariant if $T_x \Sigma$ is $D$-invariant for all $x\in \Sigma$.
Theorem 6.3 (LN, Rodríguez-Vázquez). $\g{v}\subseteq \g{p}$ invariant under the tensors $D$ and $R$. Then:
  1. $\g{s}=[\g{v},\g{v}]+\g{v}=[\g{v},\g{v}]_{\g{k}}\oplus \g{v}$ is a subalgebra.
  2. $\Sigma = \s{S}\cdot o$ is totally geodesic and $D$-invariant with $T_o \Sigma = \g{v}$.

Riemannian cones

  • $(M,g)$ complete real analytic Riemannian manifold.
  • Riemannian cone: $\widehat{M}=(0,\infty)\times_{r} M$ with the metric $$\widehat{g}=dr^2+r^2 g.$$
  • $I(\widehat{M})\cong I(M)$ (Corollary 6.20).
  • If $M^6 \neq \s{S}^6$, $\pi_1(M)=0$ and $\textup{Ric}=5g$, then (Bär '93):

    $M$ strictly nearly Kähler $\;\Rightarrow\;$ $\textup{Hol}(\widehat{M})=\s{G}_2$.

Theorem 6.25 (LN, Rodríguez-Vázquez). $\Sigma$ totally geodesic in $\widehat{M}$. Then one of the two holds:
  1. $\Sigma=\widehat{S}\,$ for $S\to M$ totally geodesic.
  2. $\Sigma$ is (up to surjective local isometry) a complete totally geodesic hypersurface in $\widehat{S}$ for $S\to M$ totally geodesic.
Theorem (Jentsch, Moroianu, Semmelmann '13). If $\textup{Hol}(M^7)=\s{G}_2$, then $M$ has no totally geodesic hypersurfaces.
Corollary D (LN, Rodríguez-Vázquez). $\widehat{M}$ cohomogeneity one
$\s{G}_2$-cone. Every maximal totally geodesic submanifold $\Sigma$ of $\widehat{M}$ is:
  1. Associative (calibrated by the $\s{G}_2$-structure $\phi$) if $\dim \Sigma = 3$.
  2. Coassociative (calibrated by $\star\phi$) if $\dim \Sigma = 4$.

Minkowski spacetime and limits

  • Minkowski spacetime $(c>0)$: $\RR^{d+1}$ with the Lorentzian inner product

    $\gamma((x,t),(y,s))=\langle x, y \rangle -c^2 ts.$

  • Galilei spacetime ($c\to \infty$): $\RR^{d+1}$ with a clock and ruler:

    $\tau(x,t)=t, \qquad \lambda((x,0),(y,0))=\langle x, y \rangle.$

  • Carroll spacetime ($c\to 0$): $\RR^{d+1}$ with

    $\kappa = (0,1), \qquad h((x,t),(y,s))=\langle x,y\rangle.$

Symmetry algebras

The symmetry algebras $\g{poin}(d)$, $\g{gal}(d)$, $\g{car}(d)$ consist of:

  • Space rotations $(x,t)\mapsto (Rx,0)$ ($R\in \g{so}(d)$).
  • Space translations $(x,t)\mapsto (a,0)$ ($a\in \RR^d$).
  • Time translations $(x,t)\mapsto (0,s)$ ($s\in \RR$).
  • Inertial boosts ($v\in \RR^d$):
    • Minkowski: $(x,t)\mapsto\bigl(tc^2v,\langle v, x \rangle\bigr)$.
    • Galilei: $(x,t)\mapsto (tv,0)$.
    • Carroll: $(x,t)\mapsto \bigl(0,\langle v, x \rangle\bigr).$

Kinematical algebras

An $(s,v)$-kinematical Lie algebra (KLA) (with $d$-dimensional space isotropy) is a real Lie algebra $\g{g}$ with:

  • $\g{so}(d)\subseteq \g{g}$,
  • $\g{g}=\g{so}(d)\oplus \bigoplus^v \RR^d \oplus \bigoplus^s \RR$ as an $\g{so}(d)$-module.

Key idea. Understand homogeneous spacetimes as quotients of kinematical groups.

Spatially isotropic homogeneous spacetimes

$M^{d+1}=\s{G}/\s{K}$ is spatially isotropic if $\g{g}$ is a $(1,2)$-KLA and the isotropy algebra is $\g{k}=\g{so}(d)\oplus \RR^d$.

  • Bacry–Lévy-Leblond ('68), Bacry–Nuyts ('86): $(1,2)$-KLAs with $d=3$.
  • Figueroa-O'Farrill ('18), Andrzejewski–Figueroa-O'Farrill ('18): $(1,2)$-KLAs with $d\geq 2$.
  • Figueroa-O'Farrill–Prohazka ('19): classification of spatially isotropic homogeneous spacetimes up to coverings.

Coisotropy one spacetimes

Structure of $(3,2)$-KLAs

  • Fix a $(3,2)$-KLA

    $\g{g}=\g{so}(V)\oplus(V\otimes W)\oplus \g{b}, \qquad V=\RR^d,\; W=\RR^2,\; \g{b}=\RR^3$.

  • The Lie bracket of $\g{g}$ is $$ \begin{aligned} \left[X,Y\right]={}& XY-YX, & [B,C]={}&{\color{red} ?}, \\ [X,v\otimes w]={}& Xv\otimes w, & [B,v\otimes w]={}&{\color{red} ?}, \\ [X,B]={}& 0, & [v_1\otimes w_1,v_2\otimes w_2]={}&{\color{red} ?}. \end{aligned} $$

Case $\dim V = d>3$

  • Key idea: the bracket $\Lambda^2\g{g}\to \g{g}$ is $\g{so}(V)$-equivariant.
  • $\Lambda^2\g{b}\to \g{b}$, so $\g{b}$ is a Bianchi algebra.
  • For each $B\in \g{b}$, $\ad(B)\colon V\otimes W \to \g{g}$ is $\g{so}(V)$-equivariant:

    $[B,v\otimes w]=v\otimes \rho(B)w, \qquad \rho \colon \g{b}\to \g{gl}(W).$

  • Bracket on $\Lambda^2(V\otimes W)$:

    $[v_1\otimes w_1,v_2\otimes w_2]=\alpha(w_1,w_2)v_1\curlywedge v_2 + \langle v_1 , v_2 \rangle \varphi(w_1\wedge w_2),$

    where $\alpha\in \s{S}^2W^*$ and $\varphi \in \textup{Hom}(\Lambda^2 W, \g{b})$.
  • Jacobi identity: $\rho$ representation, $\alpha\in \s{S}^2_{\g{b}}W^*$, $\varphi \in \textup{Hom}_{\g{b}}(\Lambda^2 W,\g{b})$ and

    $\rho(\varphi(w_1\wedge w_2))w_3=\alpha(w_2,w_3)w_1-\alpha(w_1,w_3)w_2$.

Theorem A (Figueroa-O'Farrill, LN). A $(3,2)$-KLA with $d>3$ is determined by
  1. a Bianchi algebra $\g{b}$,
  2. a two-dimensional representation $\rho\colon \g{b}\to \g{gl}(W)$,
  3. and a $2$-form $\varphi \in \Hom_{\g{b}}(\Lambda^2 W,\g{b})$.

Case $\dim V = d = 3$

  • The subspace $\g{b}$ is a Bianchi algebra.
  • Bracket of $B\in \g{b}$ with elements of $V\otimes W$:

    $[B,v\otimes w]=\lambda_B(w)(v\times\cdot) + v\otimes \rho(B)w, \qquad \lambda \in Z^1(\g{b};W^*).$

  • Jacobi identity $\rightsquigarrow$ $[\lambda]=0$ in $H^1(\g{b};W^*)$ $\rightsquigarrow$ we can assume $\lambda = 0$.
  • Bracket on $\Lambda^2(V\otimes W)$: ($d>3$) + $\sigma \in \Hom(\s{S}^2 W,W)$.

    $$ \begin{aligned} \left[v_1\otimes w_1,v_2\otimes w_2\right]={}&\alpha(w_1,w_2)v_1\curlywedge v_2+ \langle v_1 , v_2 \rangle \varphi(w_1\wedge w_2) \\[1ex] &+(v_1\times v_2)\sigma(w_1,w_2). \end{aligned} $$

  • Jacobi identity: $\rho$ representation, $\alpha$, $\varphi$, $\sigma$ invariant, and

    $ \begin{aligned} \alpha(w_2,w_3)w_1-\alpha(w_1,w_3)w_2={}&\rho(\varphi(w_1\wedge w_2))w_3\\ &+\sigma(\sigma(w_2,w_3),w_1)-\sigma(\sigma(w_1,w_3),w_2), \\[1ex] \alpha(\sigma(w_1,w_2),w_3)={}&\alpha(\sigma(w_2,w_3),w_1), \\[1ex] 0={}&\varphi(\sigma(w_1,w_2)\wedge w_3)+\textsf{cyclic}. \end{aligned} $

Theorem B (Figueroa-O'Farrill, LN). A $(3,2)$-KLA with $d=3$ is determined by
  1. a Bianchi algebra $\g{b}$,
  2. a two-dimensional representation $\rho\colon \g{b}\to \g{gl}(W)$,
  3. a $2$-form $\varphi \in \Hom_{\g{b}}(\Lambda^2 W,\g{b})$,
  4. and a bilinear $\sigma \in \Hom_\g{b}(\s{S}^2 W,W)$.

Classification of $(3,2)$-KLAs

  • $\g{g}=\g{so}(V)\oplus (V\otimes W)\oplus \g{b}, \quad \g{g}'=\g{so}(V)\oplus(V\otimes W')\oplus \g{b}'$.
  • If $d>3$, then $\g{g}\cong \g{g}'$ if and only if there exist isomorphisms $h\colon W \to W'$ and $\psi \colon \g{b}\to \g{b}'$ with

    $\rho' \circ \psi = \Ad(h)\circ \rho,\qquad\psi \circ \varphi=h^*\varphi'$.

  • If $d=3$, then $\g{g}\cong \g{g}'$ if and only if there exist $h$, $\psi$ and $\mu\in (W^*)^\g{b}$ with $$ \begin{aligned} \rho' \circ \psi ={}& \Ad(h)\circ \rho,\qquad\psi \circ \varphi=h^*\varphi' \\[1ex] h\circ \sigma ={}& h^*\sigma' + \mu \otimes h + h \otimes \mu. \end{aligned} $$
Equivalent problem. Compute for each Bianchi algebra $\g{b}$:
  1. All $2$-dimensional representations of $\g{b}$ up to weak equivalence.
  2. For each representation $\rho$:
    • The orbit space of $\Aut(\rho)\curvearrowright \Hom_{\g{b}}(\Lambda^2 W ,\g{b})$, where

      $ \begin{aligned} \Aut(\rho)={}&\bigl\{(h,\psi)\in \s{GL}(W)\times \Aut(\g{b})\colon \rho \circ \psi = \Ad(h)\circ \rho\bigr\}, \\[1ex] (h,\psi)\cdot \varphi={}&\frac{1}{\det h}(\psi \circ \varphi). \end{aligned}$

    • The orbit space of $\Aut(\rho)\ltimes (W^*)^\g{b}\curvearrowright E$, where

      $ \begin{aligned} E={}&\bigl\{ (\varphi,\sigma)\textsf{ solutions of the Jacobi identity} \bigr\},\\[1ex] (h,\psi)\cdot (\varphi,\sigma)={}& \biggl( \frac{1}{\det h}(\psi \circ \varphi),\bigl(h^{-1}\bigr)^*(h\circ \sigma-\mu\otimes h - h \otimes \mu) \biggr). \end{aligned} $

  1. Split abelian extensions (60 examples).
  2. Algebras with $\varphi \neq 0$ and $\alpha = 0$ (35 examples).
  3. Algebras with $\alpha \neq 0$ (10 examples).

Algebras with $d=3$

Theorem D (Figueroa-O'Farrill, LN). Four classes of $(3,2)$-KLAs with $d=3$ up to relative isomorphism:
  1. Split abelian extensions (60 examples).
  2. Algebras with $\varphi \neq 0$ and $\alpha,\,\sigma = 0$ (35 examples).
  3. Algebras with $\alpha \neq 0$ and $\sigma = 0$ (10 examples).
  4. Algebras with $\varphi = 0$ and $\sigma$ outside the orbit of $0$ (63 examples).
Examples in Class (IV) are exclusive to $d=3$.

Conclusions

Part I

  • Cohomogeneity two polar foliations on noncompact symmetric spaces.
  • Polar foliations on $\F\s{H}^2$ and standard polar foliations on $\H\s{H}^n$.
  • Sections of polar actions on $\F\s{H}^n$.

Part II

  • Totally geodesic submanifolds of $\C\s{P}^3$, $\s{F}(\C^3)$ and $\s{S}^3\times \s{S}^3$.
  • Totally geodesic submanifolds of Riemannian cones.
  • Maximal totally geodesic submanifolds of cohomogeneity one $\s{G}_2$-cones.

Part III

  • General expression of the Lie bracket of a $(3,2)$-KLA ($d>2$).
  • Classification of $(3,2)$-KLAs with $d>2$.
  • Classification of two-dimensional representations of Bianchi algebras.
