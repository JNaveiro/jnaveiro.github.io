2.5 Elementary Matrices

In this section we will relate the elementary row operations with matrix multiplication.

We say that a square matrix \(E\) is an elementary matrix if it is obtained from the identity matrix \(I_n\) by performing a single row operation (called the row operation corresponding to \(E\)).
The type of \(E\) is said to be the type of the corresponding row operation (so there are three types I, II and III).

For instance, let us start with the \(3\times 3\) identity matrix \(I_3\). If we apply (separately) the row operations \(R_1\leftrightarrow R_3\), \(2R_3\) and \(R_2-2R_1\), we obtain the matrices

\[ E_{R_1\leftrightarrow R_3}= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad E_{2R_3}= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad E_{R_2-2R_1}= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \]

The relationship between elementary row operations and elementary matrices is given in the following fact:

Let \(A\) be an \(m\times n\) matrix. If \(E\) is an \(m\times m\) elementary matrix, then \(EA\) is the result of applying the row operation associated with \(E\) to \(A\).
Proof. The proof of this theorem boils down to the following observation: Suppose \(E\) is an \(p\times n\) matrix and \(A\) is an \(n\times m\) matrix. If we write \(E=[e_{ij}]\) and decompose the matrix \(B\) into its rows: \[ A= \begin{bmatrix} A_1 \\ A_2 \\ \vdots \\ A_n \end{bmatrix}, \] then the decomposition of \(EA\) into rows is \[ EA= \begin{bmatrix} e_{11}A_1 + e_{12}A_2 + \dots + e_{1n}A_n \\ e_{21}A_1 + e_{22}A_2 + \dots + e_{2n}A_n \\ \vdots \\ e_{p1}A_1 + e_{m2}A_2 + \dots + e_{pn}A_n \end{bmatrix}. \]

For example, we prove the theorem for operations of type I (you can try the other types an as exercise). If we exchange the \(i\)-th and \(j\)-th rows of \(A\) ,where \(i < j \), the result is

\[ A= \begin{bmatrix} A_1 \\ \vdots \\ A_{i-1} \\ A_{j} \\ A_{i+1} \\ \vdots \\ A_{j-1} \\ A_{i} \\ A_{j+1} \\ \vdots \\ A_n \end{bmatrix}, \] Now, consider the elementary matrix \(E_{R_i \leftrightarrow R_j}\). Its entries are all zero except for: \[ e_{11}=\dots=e_{i-1,i-1}=e_{i+1,i+1}=\dots=e_{j-1,j-1}=e_{j+1,j+1}=\dots=e_{nn}=1,\quad e_{ij}=e_{ji}=1, \] So combining this with the above formula for \(EA\), we also get \[ EA= \begin{bmatrix} A_1 \\ \vdots \\ A_{i-1} \\ A_{j} \\ A_{i+1} \\ \vdots \\ A_{j-1} \\ A_{i} \\ A_{j+1} \\ \vdots \\ A_n \end{bmatrix} \] (you can see this more easily by performing this calculation with a specific example).

The previous theorem says that performing a series of elementary operations on a matrix \(A\) is the same as multiplying \(A\) by their corresponding elementary matrices.

For instance, take \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 4 & 2 \\ -2 & 0 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \] Performing the row operations \[ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 4 & 2 \\ -2 & 0 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \underset{(1/2)R_2}{\longrightarrow} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 1 \\ -2 & 0 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \underset{R_3+R_1}{\longrightarrow} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 8 \end{bmatrix} \] is the same as performing the matrix multiplication \[ E_{R_3 + R_1}(E_{(1/2) R_2}A)= E_{R_3 + R_1}\,E_{(1/2) R_2}\,A, \] or in other words: \[ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 8 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac12 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 4 & 2 \\ -2 & 0 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \]
Note the order in which we are multiplying the elementary matrices!

Inverses of Elementary Matrices

Since every row operation has a corresponding inverse row operation, it follows that every elementary matrix is invertible. More precisely:
If \(E\) is an elementary matrix corresponding to some row operation, then \(E\) is invertible and its inverse \(E^{-1}\) is precisely the elementary matrix associated with the inverse row operation.
Proof. Suppose \(F\) is the elementary matrix associated with the inverse row operation. If we start with the identity matrix \(I\), perform the row operation from \(E\), and then perform the row operation from \(F\), we come back to \(I\). This is because the row operations are inverse. In other words: \(I_n=FEI_n=FE\). Using the same argument but changing the order in which we perform the row operations, we obtain \(EF=I\). Therefore, \(E\) is invertible with inverse \(E^{-1}=F\).
The inverses of the matrices \[ E_{R_1 \leftrightarrow R_3}= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix},\quad E_{3R_3}= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix},\quad E_{R_1 + 4R_2}= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \] are, respectively: \[ E_{R_1 \leftrightarrow R_3}= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix},\quad E_{(1/3)R_3}= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \frac13 \end{bmatrix},\quad E_{R_1 - 4R_2}= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -4 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \]

More generally, the inverses of the elementary matrices are \[ E_{R_i\leftrightarrow R_j}^{-1} = E_{R_i\leftrightarrow R_j}, \quad E_{kR_i}^{-1} = E_{(1/k)R_i}, \quad E_{R_i+cR_j}^{-1} = E_{R_i-cR_j}. \]

Inverses and Elementary matrices

Suppose we carry an \(m\times n\) matrix \(A\) to another matrix \(B\) by means of some elementary row operations. For example, \(B\) may be a row-echelon form of \(A\). By applying the previous theorem as many times as the number of row operations that we are performing, we see that there exist elementary matrices \(E_1,\,E_2,\,\dots,\,E_k\) such that \(B=E_{k}E_{k-1}\cdots E_{2}E_{1}A\).

We write \(U=E_{k}E_{k-1}\cdots E_{2}E_{1}\), so that \(U\) is an invertible matrix (because it is a product of invertible matrices). The easiest way to find \(U\) is to take the identity matrix \(I_m\) and apply the same row operations (that we performed to get from \(A\) to \(B\)) to \(U\).

In fact, if we perform the same operations on the (block) matrix \([A \, \vert \, I_m]\), the result has to be \([B\, \vert \, U]\).

We gather this discussion by means of a theorem:

If an \(m\times n\) matrix \(A\) can be transformed into an \(m\times n\) matrix \(B\), then there exists an invertible \(m \times m\) matrix \(U\) such that \(B=UA\). Moreover, the same row operations that send \(A\to B\) send \([A\,\vert\, I_m]\to [B\,\vert \, U]\).
Let \[ A= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix}. \] Calculate the reduced row-echelon form \(R\) of \(A\), and find an invertible matrix \(U\) such that \(R=UA\).
Solution. Let's reduce the \(2\times 5\) matrix \([A\,\vert \, I_2]\): \[ \begin{aligned} \left[\begin{array}{ccc|cc} 1 & 2 & 3 & 1 & 0 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 & 0 & 1 \\ \end{array}\right] &\underset{R_2-4R_1}{\longrightarrow} \left[\begin{array}{ccc|cc} 1 & 2 & 3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -3 & -6 & -4 & 1 \\ \end{array}\right] \underset{-(1/3)R_2}{\longrightarrow} \left[\begin{array}{ccc|cc} 1 & 2 & 3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & -\frac43 & \frac13 \\ \end{array}\right]\\ &\underset{R_1-2R_2}{\longrightarrow} \left[\begin{array}{ccc|cc} 1 & 0 & -1 & -\frac{5}{3} & -\frac{2}{3} \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & -\frac43 & \frac13 \\ \end{array}\right]. \end{aligned} \] Thus, the desired matrices are \[ R= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix},\quad U=\frac{1}{3} \begin{bmatrix} -5 & -2 \\ -4 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \]

Now, let's apply this idea to find the inverse of a matrix. In Section 2.4, we developed (without proof) a method for determining whether a square matrix \(A\) is invertible. Now, we are going to justify said method.

Suppose that we are given a square matrix \(A\). If \(A\) is invertible, then as we mentioned in Section 2.4, we can bring \(A\) to the identity matrix \(I\) by means of some row operations. But then, from the above we know that there exists an invertible matrix \(U\) (which is a product of elementary matrices) such that \(UA=I\). As \(U\) is invertible, this gives \(A=U^{-1}\), so \(A^{-1}=U\). This means that the operations that send \(A\to I\) also send \([A\, \vert \, I]\to [I \,\vert \, A^{-1}]\), so the method that we discussed in Section 2.4 is indeed valid!

Moreover, the above paragraph shows that an invertible matrix \(A\) is automatically a product of elementary matrices. Since elementary matrices are invertible themselves, this yields:

A square matrix is invertible if and only if it is a product of elementary matrices.
In fact, this lets us say:
If \(A\) and \(B\) are both \(m\times n\) matrices, we can bring \(A\to B\) via elementary row operations if and only if there exists an invertible \(m\times m\) matrix \(U\) satisfying \(B=UA\).

We actually say in this case that \(A\) and \(B\) are row-equivalent.

Consider the matrix \[ A= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 5 \\ 1 & 11 \end{bmatrix}. \] Solve the following questions concerning \(A\):
  1. Determine \(A^{-1}\).
  2. Write \(A\) as a product of elementary matrices.
Solution. Let us find \(A^{-1}\): \[ \begin{aligned} \left[\begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 5 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 11 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right] \underset{R_2-R_1}{\longrightarrow} \left[\begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 5 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 6 & -1 & 1 \end{array}\right] \underset{(1/6)R_2}{\longrightarrow} \left[\begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 5 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac16 & \frac16 \end{array}\right] \underset{R_1-5R_2}{\longrightarrow} \left[\begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 0 & \frac{11}{6} & -\frac56 \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac16 & \frac16 \end{array}\right] \end{aligned} \] Therefore, \(A\) is invertible with inverse \[ A^{-1}= \frac16 \begin{bmatrix} 11 & -5 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} =E_{R_1-5R_2}\,E_{(1/6)R_2}\,E_{R_2-R_1}. \] Note that we have written \(A^{-1}\) as a product of elementary matrices. To write \(A\) as a product of elementary matrices, we can simply take the inverse of the above product: \[ A= (E_{R_1-5R_2}\,E_{(1/6)R_2}\,E_{R_2-R_1})^{-1} = E_{R_2+R_1}\, E_{6R_2}\, E_{R_1+5R_2}. \] In other words, \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 5 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \]
