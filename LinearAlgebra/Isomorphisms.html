Isomorphisms and Composition

As you may have noticed, even though vector spaces can "look" different from the space $\RR^n$, the practical computations that we have done in these spaces end up reducing to computations with vectors in $\RR^n$. This is because, in practice, the elements of the vector spaces that we have been working with can be "identified" with vectors in $\RR^n$.

For instance, let us consider the vector space $\bf{P}_3$. Its elements are polynomials of the form \[ a+bx+cx^2+dx^3, \qquad a,\,b,\,c,\,d\in\RR. \] Notice that we can identify polynomials in $\bf{P}_3$ with vectors in $\RR^4$ by sending \[ a+bx+cx^2+dx^3 \to (a,b,c,d) \] In addition, polynomial addition and scalar multiplication correspond to vector addition and scalar multiplication under this identification. This means that, in some sense, the space $\bf{P}_3$ is the same as the space $\RR^4$. In this section, we make this idea of "being the same" precise. In addition, we will see that every vector space can be identified with $\RR^n$ for an adequate value of $n$.

Isomorphisms between Vector Spaces

Let $V$ and $W$ be two vector spaces. We say that a linear transformation $T\colon V\to W$ is an isomorphism if it is both one-to-one and onto. Moreover, we say that $V$ and $W$ are isomorphic (denoted $V\cong W$) if there exists an isomorphism $T\colon V\to W$.

Let us go through some examples:

If $V$ is a vector space, then the identity transformation $1_V\colon V\to V$ is an isomorphism.
The transposition $T\colon\bf{M}_{mn}\to\bf{M}_{nm}$ (given by $T(A)=A^t$) is an isomorphism, so $\bf{M}_{mn}\cong\bf{M}_{nm}$.
The space $\bf{P}_3$ is isomorphic to $\RR^4$. Indeed, the transformation $T\colon \bf{P}_3\to \RR^4$ given by \[ T\big(a+bx+cx^2+dx^3\big)=(a,b,c,d) \] is an isomorphism. Observe that $T(1)=\bf{e}_1$, $T(x)=\bf{e}_2$, $T\big(x^2\big)=\bf{e}_3$ and $T\big(x^3\big)=\bf{e}_4$, so that $T$ sends the basis $\big\{1,x,x^2,x^3\big\}$ to the standard basis of $\RR^4$.

The existence of a vector space isomorphism $T\colon V\to W$ implies that we can work with vectors of $V$ or $W$ indistinctively. That is because each vector $\bf{v}\in V$ can be uniquely identified with $T(\bf{v})\in W$. Therefore, isomorphic vector spaces are identical in practice.

In general, how do we determine if two vector spaces are isomorphic? It all comes down to checking their dimension:

Let $V$ and $W$ be two finite-dimensional vector spaces, $T\colon V \to W$ a linear transformation and $\{\bf{e}_1,\bf{e}_2,\dots,\bf{e}_n\}$ a basis for $V$. Then the following conditions are equivalent:
  1. $T\colon V\to W$ is an isomorphism.
  2. The set $\{T(\bf{e}_1),T(\bf{e}_2),\dots,T(\bf{e}_n)\}$ is a basis of $W$.
Proof.

Suppose $T$ is an isomorphism, and let us prove that $B=\{T(\bf{e}_1,),T(\bf{e}_2),\dots,T(\bf{e}_n)\}$ is a basis of $W$.

Firstly, $B$ is a spanning set for $W$. Indeed, since $\{\bf{e}_1,\bf{e}_2,\dots,\bf{e}_n\}$ is a spanning set for $V$, we have \[ \im T = \vecspan\{T(\bf{e}_1),T(\bf{e}_2),\dots,T(\bf{e}_n)\} = \vecspan\, B. \] Secondly, $B$ is linearly independent. Indeed, suppose we have a linear combination \[ a_1T(\bf{e}_1)+a_2T(\bf{e}_2)+\cdots+a_nT(\bf{e}_n)=\bf{0} \] Then, since $T$ is linear, we have \[ T(a_1\bf{e}_1+a_2\bf{e}_2+\cdots+a_n\bf{e}_n)=\bf{0}, \] so the expression in parenthesis lives in $\ker T = \{\bf{0}\}$ (since $T$ is one-to-one). Therefore, \[ a_1\bf{e}_1+a_2\bf{e}_2+\cdots+a_n\bf{e}_n=\bf{0}, \] and since $\{\bf{e}_1,\bf{e}_2,\dots,\bf{e}_n\}$ is a basis of $V$, this gives $a_1=a_2=\cdots=a_n=0$, showing that $B$ is linearly independent. We conclude that $B$ is a basis of $W$.

Now, suppose $B=\{T(\bf{e}_1),T(\bf{e_2}),\dots,T(\bf{e}_n)\}$ is a basis for $W$, and let us prove that $T$ is an isomorphism.

To see if $T$ is one-to-one, we compute $\ker T$. Suppose $\bf{x}\in \ker T$, and let us write \[ \bf{x}=a_1\bf{e}_1+a_2\bf{e}_2+\cdots+a_n\bf{e}_n. \] Then $T(\bf{x})=\bf{0}$, so \[ a_1T(\bf{e}_1)+a_2T(\bf{e}_2)+\cdots+a_nT(\bf{e}_n)=\bf{0}, \] and the fact that $B$ is independent gives $a_1=a_2=\cdots=a_n=0$. Consequently, $\bf{x}=\bf{0}$, so $\ker T = \{\bf{0}\}$.

The transformation $T$ is also onto, since \[ \im T = \vecspan \{T(\bf{e})_1,T(\bf{e}_2),\dots,T(\bf{e}_n)\}=W. \] We conclude that $T$ is an isomorphism.

If $V$ and $W$ are two finite-dimensional vector spaces, then $V$ and $W$ are isomorphic if and only if they have the same dimension: $\dim V = \dim W$.
Proof.

Because of the previous theorem, two isomorphic vector spaces must have the same dimension. We have to prove the converse assertion.

Assume $V$ and $W$ have the same dimension $\dim V = \dim W = n$. We choose a basis $\{\bf{e}_1,\bf{e}_2,\dots,\bf{e}_n\}$ of $V$ and a basis $\{\bf{u}_1,\bf{u}_2,\dots,\bf{u}_n\}$ of $W$. Because of the existence and uniqueness theorem, there exists a linear transformation $T\colon V\to W$ such that $T(\bf{e}_i)=\bf{u}_i$ for each $i$. This transformation takes a basis of $V$ to a basis of $W$ by design, so it is an isomorphism due to the previous theorem.

An important consequence of this theorem is that every vector space can be identified with some $\RR^n$:

Let $V$ be a finite dimensional vector space and $n=\dim V$. Then we have $V\cong \RR^n$.

Now, we know that in order for an isomorphism $T\colon V \to W$ to even exist, we must have $\dim V = \dim W$. Therefore, if we are given a transformation $T\colon V \to W$ and we want to check if $T$ is an isomorphism, a first thing to look at is if $\dim V = \dim W$.

Using the dimension theorem, we can simplify the process of determining if a linear transformation is an isomorphism.

Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces of the same dimension and $T\colon V \to W$ a linear transformation. Then the following statements are equivalent:
  1. $T\colon V \to W$ is one-to-one.
  2. $T\colon V \to W$ is onto.
  3. $T\colon V \to W$ is an isomorphism.
Proof.

Recall that $\dim (\ker T) + \dim (\im T)=\dim V$ by the dimension theorem. Therefore, we get \[ \begin{aligned} T\textsf{ is one-to-one} &\iff \ker T = \{\bf{0}\} \iff \dim (\ker T) = 0 \iff \dim (\im T) = \dim V = \dim W \\ &\iff \im T = W \iff T\textsf{ is onto.} \end{aligned} \] This proves that the three assertions are equivalent.

Consider the linear transformation $T\colon \bf{M}_{22}\to\RR^4$ given by \[ T \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} = (a+b,d,c,a-b). \] Check if $T$ is an isomorphism.
Solution.

Since $\bf{M}_{22}$ and $\RR^4$ both have dimension $4$, it is enough to check if $T$ is one-to-one or onto.

Let us check if $T$ is one-to-one by seeing if $\ker T = \{\bf{0}\}$. For this, take any matrix \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\in\bf{M}_{22}. \] Then $A\in\ker T$ if $T(A)=(a+b,d,c,a-b)=(0,0,0,0)$, which is the same as the coefficients $a,\,b,\,c,\,d$ satisfying the homogeneous linear system \[ \left\{ \begin{array}{*{9}{c}} a & + & b & & & & & = & 0 \\ & & & & & & d & = & 0 \\ & & & & c & & & = & 0 \\ a & - & b & & & & & = & 0 \end{array} \right. \]

This system only has the trivial solution $a=b=c=d=0$, so we see that $A=0$. Therefore, $\ker T = \{\bf{0}\}$, which shows that $T$ is one-to-one. Using the fact that $\dim\bf{M}_{22}=\dim\RR^4=4$, we deduce that $T$ is an isomorphism.

Is the subspace \[ U = \big\{p(x)\in\bf{P}_4\mid p(2)=0 \big\}\subseteq \bf{P}_4 \] isomorphic to $\bf{M}_{22}$? If so, find an isomorphism $T\colon \bf{M}_{22} \to U$.
Solution.

As we had seen in a previous example, $\dim U=4$ and the set \[ \left\{x-2,x^2-4,x^3-8,x^4-16\right\} \] is a basis of $U$. Since $\dim\bf{M}_{22}=4$ as well, we conclude that $U$ and $\bf{M}_{22}$ are isomorphic.

To find an isomorphism $T\colon U \to \bf{M}_{22}$, recall that the set \[ \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \right\} \] is a basis of $\bf{M}_{22}$, and the existence and uniqueness theorem states that there exists a unique linear transformation $T\colon \bf{M}_{22} \to U$ that satisfies \[ \begin{aligned} T \left( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \right) &= x-2, & T \left( \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \right) &= x^2-4, \\[1em] T \left( \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \right) &= x^3-8, & T \left( \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \right) &= x^4-16, \end{aligned} \]

Explicitly, this transformation is given by \[ \begin{aligned} T\left( \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \right) &= a(x-2)+b\big(x^2-4\big)+c\big(x^3-8\big)+d\big(x^4-16\big) \\[1em] &= dx^4+cx^3+bx^2-(2a+4b+8c+16d). \end{aligned} \]

Composition of Linear Transformations

Suppose $U,\,V$ and $W$ are vector spaces and we are given transformations $T\colon U\to V$ and $S\colon V\to W$. Then, for each vector $\bf{u}\in U$, we can apply $T$ to obtain a vector $T(\bf{u})\in V$, and we can now apply $S$ to this vector to obtain a vector $S(T(\bf{u}))\in W$. This leads to the notion of composing transformations:

Suppose $T\colon U\to V$ and $S\colon V\to W$ are transformations between vector spaces. The composite of $T$ and $S$ is the transformation $ST\colon U \to W$ given by the formula \[ ST(\bf{u})=S(T(\bf{u})), \qquad \bf{u}\in U. \]
Not all pairs of transformations can be composed. For instance, if we have two linear transformations $T\colon \RR^2\to\RR^3$ and $S\colon \RR^3 \to \RR^4$, then $ST$ exists and is a transformation from $\RR^2$ to $\RR^4$, whereas $TS$ does not exist because we cannot apply $T$ to vectors in $\RR^4$.
In addition, even if both $ST$ and $TS$ exist, it may be the case that $ST\neq TS$, as we have already seen in the case of matrix transformations.
Consider the matrix \[ J = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}. \] We define $T\colon\bf{M}_{22}\to\bf{M}_{22}$ by $T(A)=A^t$ and $S\colon \bf{M}_{22}\to\bf{M}_{22}$ by $S(A)=AJ$. Compute $ST$ and $TS$.
Solution.

The transformation $ST$ is given by \[ ST \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} = S \left( \begin{bmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{bmatrix} \right) = \begin{bmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} c & -a \\ d & -b \end{bmatrix} \]

Meanwhile, the transformation $TS$ is given by \[ TS \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} = T \left(\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \right) = T \begin{bmatrix} b & -a \\ d & -c \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} b & d \\ -a & -c \end{bmatrix}. \]

Note how $ST\neq TS$ in this example.

The main properties of composite transformations are collected below. It is a good exercise to prove them.

Suppose we are given three transformations $T\colon U \to V$, $S\colon V\to W$ and $R\colon W\to X$, where $U,\,V,\,W$ and $X$ are vector spaces.
  1. $(RS)T=R(ST)$ (that is, composition is associative).
  2. $R1_{V}=1_WR=R$.
  3. If $T$ and $S$ are linear, then $ST$ is linear (that is, the composite of linear transformations is also linear).

The relationship between isomorphisms and composite transformations is that the linear transformations that admit an inverse (with respect to composition) are precisely the ones that are isomorphisms.

Let $T\colon V\to W$ be a linear transformation between finite dimensional spaces. Then the following statements are equivalent:
  1. $T\colon V\to W$ is an isomorphism.
  2. There exists a transformation $S\colon W\to V$ such that $ST=1_V$ and $TS=1_W$.
In addition, in this case $S$ is also an isomorphism, it is unique, and it can be computed as follows: for each $\bf{w}\in W$, $S(\bf{w})$ is the unique solution to the equation $T(\bf{x})=\bf{w}$.
Proof.

Let us first assume that $T$ is an isomorphism. If we choose a basis $\{\bf{e}_1,\bf{e}_2,\dots,\bf{e}_n\}$ of $V$, then the set $\{T(\bf{e}_1),T(\bf{e}_2),\dots,T(\bf{e}_n)\}$ is also a basis of $W$ because $T$ carries bases of $V$ to bases of $W$. Now, we can use the existence and uniqueness theorem to guarantee that there exists a linear transformation $S\colon W \to V$ such that \[ S(T(\bf{e}_i))=\bf{e}_i \qquad \textsf{for all } i. \] Note that $ST=1_V$ because they agree on a basis of $V$. To check that $TS=1_W$, we note that \[ TS(T(\bf{e}_i))=T((ST)(\bf{e}_i))=T(\bf{e}_i) \qquad \textsf{for all } i. \] Therefore, $TS=1_{W}$ because they agree on a basis of $W$. Also, observe that $S$ is an isomorphism because it carries a basis of $W$ to a basis of $V$.

We check that the transformation $S$ defined above is unique and defined by the condition that we described above. Take any $\bf{w}\in W$, and consider the equation $T(\bf{x})=\bf{w}$ (with unknown $\bf{x}\in V$). Then $\bf{x}=S(\bf{w})$ is a solution because $T(S(\bf{w}))=\bf{w}$. In addition, if $\bf{y}\in V$ is another solution (that is, $T(\bf{y})=\bf{w}$), we see that \[ \bf{y}=ST(\bf{y})=S(T(\bf{y}))=S(\bf{w}). \] Thus, the equation $T(\bf{x})=\bf{w}$ has the unique solution $\bf{x}=S(\bf{w})$, which also shows that $S$ is unique.

We now prove the converse: assume $T$ admits a transformation $S\colon W\to V$ with $ST=1_V$ and $TS=1_W$, and let us show that $T$ is an isomorphism.

Firstly, $T$ is one-to-one. Indeed, if $\bf{x}\in \ker T$, then we have \[ \bf{x}=ST(\bf{x})=S(T(\bf{x}))=S(\bf{0})=\bf{0}, \] so $\ker T =\{\bf{0}\}$, which implies that $T$ is one-to-one.

Secondly, $T$ is onto. This is because if $\bf{w}\in W$ is any vector, then $\bf{w}=T(S(\bf{w}))$, so $\bf{w}\in \im T$, which yields $\im T = W$.

We thus conclude that $T$ is an isomorphism.
