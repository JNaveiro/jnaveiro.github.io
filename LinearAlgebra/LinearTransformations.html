2.6 Linear Transformations

In this section we study the transformations \(T\colon \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\) that behave well with respect to the operations of addition and scalar multiplication. To motivate what this should mean, let us consider some matrix transformation \[ T_A \colon \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m, \] where \(A\) is an \(m\times n\) matrix. From the general properties of matrix-vector multiplication, we see that if \(\mathbf{x},\,\mathbf{y}\) are vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and \(a\) is a scalar, then

\[ \begin{aligned} T_A(\bf{x}+\bf{y})&= A(\bf{x}+\bf{y}) =A\bf{x} + A\bf{y} =T_A(\bf{x})+T_A(\bf{y}), \\ T_A(a\bf{x})&= A(a\bf{x}) =a(A\bf{x}) =aT_A(\bf{x}). \end{aligned} \]
We say that a transformation \(T\colon \RR^n \to \RR^m\) is linear if for all vectors \(\bf{x},\,\bf{y}\) in \(\RR^n\) and all scalars \(a\) we have:
  1. \(T(\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y})=T(\mathbf{x})+T(\mathbf{y})\),
  2. \(T(a\bf{x})=aT(\bf{x})\).
The equations from the beginning show that for any \(m\times n\) matrix \(A\), the corresponding matrix transformation \(T_A\colon \RR^n \to \RR^m\) is linear.

Linear Transformations and Linear Combinations

By definition, linear transformations are going to preserve both properties of addition and scalar multiplication. If we combine these two operations, this amounts to taking linear combinations. Therefore, it makes sense to expect that linear transformations should also preserve linear combinations in some way.

If $T\colon \RR^n \to \RR^n$ is a linear transformation, then for any set of vectors $\bf{x}_1,\,\dots,\,\bf{x}_k$ and scalars $a_1\,\dots,\,a_k$, we have \[ T(a_1\bf{x}_1 + \cdots + a_k \bf{x}_k)=a_1 T(\bf{x_1})+\cdots + a_k T(\bf{x}_k). \]
Proof. For $k=1$, this is just the second equation from the definition. Let us prove this for $k=2$ and $k=3$. In the case $k=2$, using the definition of linearity we see that \[ T(a_1 \bf{x}_1 + a_2 \bf{x}_2) = T(a_1\bf{x}_1) + T(a_2 \bf{x}_2) = a_1 T(\bf{x}_1) + a_2 T(\bf{x}_2). \] The case $k=3$ is done in the same way, but it takes a bit longer: \[ \begin{aligned} T(a_1 \bf{x}_1 +a_2 \bf{x}_2 + a_3 \bf{x}_3) &= T(a_1 \bf{x}_1 + a_2 \bf{x}_2) + T(a_3 \bf{x}_3) = T(a_1 \bf{x}_1) + T(a_2 \bf{x}_2) + T(a_3 \bf{x}_3) \\ &= a_1 T(\bf{x}_1) + a_2 T(\bf{x}_2) + a_3 T(\bf{x}_3). \end{aligned} \] The general case is proved in the same way.

Because of this theorem, if we know the image of some vectors $\bf{x}_1,\,\dots,\,\bf{x}_k$ under a linear transformation $T$, we automatically know the image of any of their linear combinations!

Suppose $T\colon \RR^3 \to \RR^2$ is a linear transformation that satisfies \[ T\left( \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \right) = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}, \quad T\left( \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 2\\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \right) = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}. \] Find the image of $(3,-4,9)$ under $T$.
Solution. If we try to express $(3,-4,9)$ as a linear combination of $(1,0,1)$ and $(0,2,-3)$, we obtain \[ \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -4 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix} = 3 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} -2 \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 2 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}. \] Now, because linear transformations preserve linear combinations, this means that \[ T\left(\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -4 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix}\right) = 3 T\left(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\right) -2 T\left(\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 2 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}\right) =3 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} -2 \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix}. \]

Linear Transformations are Matrix Transformations

As we just saw, any matrix transformation $T_A$ turns out to be linear. We could ask ourselves the following question: do there exist linear transformations $T\colon \RR^n \to \RR^m$ that are not matrix transformations? Or is it the case that all linear transformations have to be matrix transformations?

Recall that we defined the standard basis vectors $\bf{e}_1,\,\bf{e_2},\,\dots,\,\bf{e}_n$ of $\RR^n$. These were precisely the columns of the identity matrix $I_n$, so that $I_n=[\bf{e}_1\;\cdots\;\bf{e}_n]$. We will say that the standard basis of $\RR^n$ is the set $\{\bf{e}_1,\,\dots,\,\bf{e}_n\}$.

Now, suppose $T\colon \RR^n \to \RR^m$ is a linear transformation. If $\bf{x}=(x_1,\,\dots,\,x_n)$ is any vector in $\RR^n$, we can see that \[ \bf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{bmatrix} = x_1 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + x_2 \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} +\dots+ x_n \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} =x_1 \bf{e_1} + x_2 \bf{e}_2 + \cdots + x_n \bf{e}_n. \]

Now, since linear transformations preserve linear combinations, if we apply $T$ to the above equation we obtain \[ T(\bf{x}) = T(x_1 \bf{e}_1 + x_2 \bf{e}_2 + \cdots + x_n \bf{e}_n) = x_1 T(\bf{e}_1) + x_2 T(\bf{e}_2) + \cdots + x_n T(\bf{e}_n). \]

The right hand side is precisely the multiplication of the $m\times n$ matrix $A=[T(\bf{e}_1)\,\cdots\, T(\bf{e}_n)]$ with the vector $\bf{x}$. In other words, we have just shown that \[ T(\bf{x})=A\bf{x}=T_A(\bf{x}) \] for every $\bf{x}$ in $\RR^n$. Summarizing,

Let $T\colon \RR^n \to \RR^m$ be a transformation. The following statements hold:
  1. $T$ is a linear transformation if and only if it is a matrix transformation.
  2. If $T$ is a linear transformation, then $T=T_A$ is induced by the matrix \[ A=[T(\bf{e}_1)\,\cdots \, T(\bf{e}_n)]. \]

Because of all this, we can now use the terms linear transformation and matrix transformation indistinctively. Furthermore, for a linear transformation $T$, we will say that the matrix of $T$ is the matrix $A$ that satisfies $T=T_A$.

Composition of Linear Transformations

Let $T\colon \RR^n \to \RR^m$ and $S\colon \RR^m \to \RR^p$ be two linear transformations. Then, the composition $S\circ T \colon \RR^n \to \RR^p$ is also a linear transformation.
Proof. Because linear transformations are matrix transformations, there exist an $m\times n$ matrix $A$ and an $p \times n$ matrix $B$ such that $T=T_A$ and $S=T_B$. Therefore, $S\circ T = T_B \circ T_A = T_{BA}$, which is a matrix transformation. Since matrix transformations are linear transformations, we deduce that $S \circ T$ is a linear transformation as well.

Examples of Linear Transformations

Suppose $\theta$ is an angle (measured in radians). The rotation $R_{\theta}\colon \RR^2 \to \RR^2$ is the transformation such that, for each $2$-vector $\bf{x}$, $R_{\theta}(\bf{x})$ is the result of rotating $\bf{x}$ counterclockwise by an angle of $\theta$ radians. This transformation is linear, and its matrix is \[ \begin{bmatrix} \cos \theta & -\sin \theta \\ \sin \theta & \cos \theta \end{bmatrix}. \]
Let $\theta$ be an angle strictly between $-\pi$ and $\pi$, and consider the line $L$ in $\RR^2$ that forms an an angle of $\theta$ radians with respect to the $x$ axis. The equation of this line is $y=(\tan \theta) x$.
The projection $P_\theta \colon \RR^2 \to \RR^2$ is the linear transformation that sends each vector $\bf{x}$ in $\RR^2$ to the point $T(\bf{x})$ that lies inside $L$ and is closest to $\bf{x}$.
  1. Show that $P_0$ is a linear transformation and find its matrix.
  2. Now, do the same thing for any angle $\theta$.
Solution. First, if $\theta = 0$, then $L$ is the line with equation $y=0$, and it is not hard to see geometrically that \[ P_0 \left( \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} \right) = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}. \] Therefore, $P_0$ is a matrix (thus, linear) transformation with matrix \[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}. \] Now, let's argue the general case. To project a vector $\bf{x}$ onto the line $y=(\tan \theta)x$, we can perform the following three steps:
  1. Perform a rotation $R_{-\theta}$ of angle $-\theta$.
  2. Project the resulting vector $R_{-\theta}(\bf{x})$ onto the line $y=0$ (the result of rotating $L$ by an angle of $-\theta$ radians).
  3. Rotate everything back by an angle of $\theta$ radians (that is, apply $R_{\theta}$).
In other words, we are saying that the projection $P_{\theta}$ is the same as the composition $R_{\theta}\circ P_0 \circ R_{-\theta}$. Therefore, $P_{\theta}$ is a linear transformation because it is the composition of linear transformations, and its matrix is \[ \begin{aligned} \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos(-\theta) & -\sin(-\theta) \\ \sin(-\theta) & \cos(-\theta) \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}\\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \\&= \begin{bmatrix} \cos^2 \theta & \sin\theta \cos \theta \\ \sin\theta \cos\theta & \sin^2\theta \end{bmatrix}. \end{aligned} \]
Let $\theta$ and $L$ be as in the previous example. The reflection $Q_\theta \colon \RR^2 \to \RR^2$ is the transformation that sends each $\bf{x}$ in $\RR^2$ to its mirror image with respect to the line $L$. Following the same procedure as with the projection $P_\theta$, show that $Q_\theta$ is linear and find its matrix.
Solution. First, let us assume $\theta = 0$, so that $L$ is simply the $x$ axis. If $\bf{x}=(x,y)$ is any vector in the plane, then its reflection along the $x$ axis is given by \[ Q_0(\bf{x}) = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ -y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}, \] thus showing that $Q_0$ is linear. Arguing as with the previous case, we can see that \[ Q_\theta = R_\theta \circ Q_0 \circ R_{-\theta}, \] meaning that $Q_\theta$ is a linear transformation because it is the composition of two linear transformations. Its matrix is \[ \begin{aligned} \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos(-\theta) & -\sin(-\theta) \\ \sin(-\theta) & \cos(-\theta) \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}\\ &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ \sin \theta & -\cos \theta \end{bmatrix} \\&= \begin{bmatrix} \cos^2 \theta -\sin^2 \theta & 2\sin\theta \cos \theta \\ 2\sin\theta \cos\theta & \sin^2\theta-\cos^2\theta \end{bmatrix}. \end{aligned} \]
