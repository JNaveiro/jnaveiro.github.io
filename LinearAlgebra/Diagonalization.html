3.3 Diagonalization and Eigenvalues

In many practical problems that are tackled with linear algebra, there is a recurring need in computing large powers of a square matrix $A$. However, computing such powers can be a very costly operation, due to the amount of additions and multiplications that are involved in calculating a matrix product. Because of this, it is crucial to find a way to caltulate these powers in an efficient way.

The Fibonacci sequence is the sequence $F_0,\,F_1,\,F_2,\,\dots$ that is obtained by setting $F_0=0$, $F_1=1$, and the next terms are computed according to the following rule: \[ F_{n}=F_{n-1}+F_{n-2}, \qquad n>1. \] The relationship above can be written as a vector equation: \[ \begin{bmatrix} F_{n} \\ F_{n-1} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} F_{n-1} \\ F_{n-2} \end{bmatrix} \] Applying this equation $n-1$ times, we obtain that \[ \begin{bmatrix} F_{n} \\ F_{n-1} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}^{n-1} \begin{bmatrix} F_{1} \\ F_{0} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}^{n-1} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \] This means that, if we want to find a formula for $F_n$, we need to find a formula for the powers of \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}. \]
If $A$ is a square matrix, we define the matrix exponential $e^A$ as the "power series" \[ \begin{aligned} e^A&= I+A+\frac{1}{2!}A^{2}+\frac{1}{3!}A^{3}+\frac{1}{4!}A^{4}+\,... \\&= \lim_{k\to \infty} \bigg( I+A+\frac{1}{2!}A^{2}+\frac{1}{3!}A^{3}+\frac{1}{4!}A^{4}+\,...+\frac{1}{k!}A^{k} \bigg). \end{aligned} \] The matrix exponential appears, for instance, in solving linear systems of Ordinary Differential Equations (ODE), and its computation involves (theoretically) knowing all the powers of $A$.

There are two key observations that will allow us to solve problems like the ones from these examples. The first one is that computing powers of diagonal matrices is particularly easy, since if $D$ is a diagonal (square) matrix, then the power $D^k$ is the diagonal matrix whose diagonal entries are the $k$-th powers of those of $D$. For instance, \[ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}^{3} = \begin{bmatrix} 8 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 27 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}. \]

The second one is that, given a matrix $A$, if we were somehow able to find an invertible matrix $P$ and a diagonal matrix $D$ such that $A=PDP^{-1}$, then we could compute the powers of $A$ by the formula \[ A^{k}=PD^{k}P^{-1}. \]

Our objective in this section is to develop a technique to find the matrices $P$ and $D$. This technique is known as diagonalization.

Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Let $A$ be an $n\times n$ square matrix. We say that a real number $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $A$ if there exists a nonzero vector $\bf{x}\neq \bf{0}$ in $\RR^n$ such that \[ A\bf{x}=\lambda \bf{x}. \] The vector $\bf{x}$ is called an eigenvector of $A$ associated with the eigenvalue $\lambda$ (or simply an $\lambda$-eigenvector).

In general, how can we find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a square matrix $A$? Observe that if $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $A$ and $\bf{x}$ is an eigenvector of $A$ associated with $\lambda$, then we have \[ A\bf{x}=\lambda \bf{x} \Rightarrow \bf{0}=\lambda\bf{x} - A\bf{x} = (\lambda I - A) \bf{x}. \] This means that a number $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $A$ if and only if the homogeneous system $(\lambda I - A)\bf{x}=\bf{0}$ as a nontrivial solution. From the results in Section 2.4, we know that this last condition is equivalent to $\lambda I - A$ not being invertible, and in terms of determinants, this means that $\det(\lambda I - A)=0$.

If $A$ is an $n\times n$ matrix, we define a function $c_A\colon \RR \to \RR$ by the equation \[ c_{A}(x)=\det(xI-A). \] This function is a polynomial of degree $n$, known as the characteristic polynomial of $A$.

From the discussion above, we arrive at the following result:

Let $A$ be an $n\times n$ matrix.
  • A number $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $A$ if and only if $c_{A}(\lambda) = 0$.
  • If $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $A$, then the $\lambda$-eigenvectors of $A$ are the nontrivial solutions to the homogeneous system \[(\lambda I-A)\bf{x}=\bf{0}.\]

Recall that for a homogeneous system $B\bf{x}=\bf{0}$, we had a notion of basic solutions. If $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of the square matrix $A$, then we will say that the basic eigenvectors of $A$ associated with $\lambda$ are the basic solutions of the homogeneous system $(\lambda I - A)\bf{x}=\bf{0}$.

Calculate the characteristic polynomial, the eigenvalues and the basic eigenvectors of \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \]
Solution.

The characteristic polynomial of $A$ is \[ \begin{aligned} c_A(x) &= \left\lvert \begin{array}{ccc} x-1 & -2 & -3 \\ 0 & x & -2 \\ 0 & -1 & x-1 \end{array} \right\rvert = (x-1)(x^{2}-x-2) = (x-1)(x+1)(x-2). \end{aligned} \] Therefore, there are three eigenvalues for $A$, which are $\lambda = 1$, $\lambda = -1$ and $\lambda = 2$.

Let us compute the basic eigenvectors of $A$ associated with $\lambda = 1$. This amounts to solving the homogeneous system whose coefficient matrix is $I-A$, which is \[ \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -2 & -3 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}. \] Using Gaussian elimination (or direct inspection in this example), one sees that the $1$-eigenvectors of $A$ are of the form \[ \bf{x}=t \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad t \textsf{ arbitrary}. \] Thus, a set of basic eigenvectors with basic eigenvalue $1$ is given by the single vector \[ \bf{x}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}. \]
