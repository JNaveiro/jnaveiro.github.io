5.5 Similarity and Diagonalization

In this section we revisit diagonalization of square matrices using the language of vector subspaces and bases.

Similar Matrices

Suppose $A$ and $B$ are square matrices of the same size. We say that $A$ and $B$ are similar (denoted $A\sim B$) if there exists an invertible matrix $P$ such that \[ B=P^{-1}AP. \]

The similarity relation satisfies the following three properties:

  1. $A\sim A$ (because $A=I^{-1}AI$).
  2. If $A\sim B$, then $B\sim A$ (because $B=P^{-1}AP$ with $P$ invertible implies $A=PBP^{-1}$).
  3. If $A\sim B$ and $B\sim C$ then $A\sim C$ (because $B=P^{-1}AP$ and $C=Q^{-1}BQ$ for invertible matrices $P,\,Q$ implies $C=(PQ)^{-1}A(PQ)$, with $PQ$ invertible).
It is common to say because of this that similarity is an equivalence relation.

With this language, we can restate notion of diagonalizability as follows: A square matrix $A$ is diagonalizable if it is similar to a diagonal matrix.

If $A$ is a diagonalizable square matrix, then every matrix that is similar to $A$ is also diagonalizable.
Solution.

If $A$ is diagonalizable, then $A\sim D$ for some diagonal matrix $D$. If $B$ is a square matrix such that $B\sim A$, then $B\sim D$ from the above properties, so $B$ is also diagonalizable.

If $A,\,B$ are square matrices and $A\sim B$, show that
  1. $A^{T}\sim B^{T}$.
  2. If $A$ is invertible, then $B$ is invertible and $A^{-1}\sim B^{-1}$.
  3. $A^{k}\sim B^{k}$ for all $k=1,2,\dots$
Solution.

Since $A\sim B$, we know that there exists an invertible matrix $P$ such that $B=P^{-1}AP$. We will use this fact to show that the three above items are true.

Firstly, by transposing the identity $B=P^{-1}AP$, we obtain $B^{T}=P^{T}A^{T}(P^{-1})^{T}=P^{T}A^{T}\big(P^{T}\big)^{-1}$, which shows that $A^{T}$ and $B^{T}$ are similar matrices.

Secondly, since $B=P^{-1}AP$ and both $A$ and $P$ are invertible, it follows that $B$ is invertible as a product of invertible matrices. Moreover, by inverting the previous equation we get $B^{-1}=P^{-1}A^{-1}P$, so $A^{-1}$ and $B^{-1}$ are similar matrices.

Finally, if we take the equation $B=P^{-1}AP$ and square both sides, we obtain $B^{2}=P^{-1}APP^{-1}AP=P^{-1}A^{2}P$, so $A^{2}$ and $B^{2}$ are similar matrices. For higher powers, the argument is the same.

It turns out that similar matrices will share many properties. For instance, if $A\sim B$, then $\det(A)=\det(B)$. This is because if $B=P^{-1}AP$ for some invertible matrix $P$, we get \[ \det(B)=\det\big(P^{-1}\big)\det(A)\det(P)=\frac{1}{\det(P)}\det(A)\det(P) = \det(A). \] There is another function that, while we will not be using it much throughout the course, it appears in several contexts:

If $A$ is an $n\times n$ matrix, then the trace of $A$ (denoted $\tr(A)$) is defined as the sum of the elements in its main diagonal. Explicitly, if $A=[a_{ij}]$, then $\tr(A)=a_{11}+a_{22}+\dots+a_{nn}$.

For example: \[ \tr \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 & 9 \end{bmatrix} = 1+5+9 = 15. \] One can show by direct computation that \[ \tr(AB)=\tr(BA) \quad \textsf{for all square matrices } A,\,B. \]

If $A$ and $B$ are similar matrices, then they have the same determinant, trace, characteristic polynomial and eigenvalues.
Proof.

We already showed that $A$ and $B$ have the same determinant, so we have to focus on the other three items. As $A$ and $B$ are similar, we can write $B=P^{-1}AP$ for some invertible matrix $P$. Taking the trace of the above equation we get \[ \tr(B)=\tr\big(P^{-1}AP\big)=\tr\big(APP^{-1}\big)=\tr(A). \] Let us now find the characteristic polynomial of $B$: \[ \begin{aligned} c_{B}(x)&=\det(xI-B)=\det\big(x\big(P^{-1}IP\big)-P^{-1}AP\big) = \det\big(P^{-1}(xI-A)P\big)\\ &= \det\big(P^{-1}\big)\det(xI-A)\det(P)=\det(xI-A)=c_{A}(x). \end{aligned} \] We conclude from the above equation that $A$ and $B$ have the same characteristic polynomial, and since the eigenvalues of a matrix coincide with the roots of the characteristic polynomial, we also conclude that $A$ and $B$ have the same eigenvalues.

The converse of the above theorem is not true. For instance, the matrices \[ I= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix},\qquad B= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \] have the same determinant, trace, characteristic polynomial and eigenvalues, but they are not similar, because if $P$ is any invertible matrix we have $P^{-1}IP=I\neq B$.

Diagonalization

Recall that an $n\times n$ matrix $A$ is diagonalizable if it is similar to a diagonal matrix $D$. We also say that a matrix $P$ satisfying $D=P^{-1}AP$ is a diagonalizing matrix for $A$.

If $P$ is a diagonalizing matrix for $A$, then we saw (c.f. Section 3.3) that the columns of $P$ are eigenvectors of $A$ whose eigenvalues were the corresponding diagonal entries of $D$. Now, the fact that $P$ is invertible is equivalent to the columns of $P$ being a basis of $\RR^n$. Therefore, we have:

Let $A$ be an $n\times n$ matrix.
  1. $A$ is diagonalizable if and only if there exists a basis $\{\bf{x}_1,\dots,\bf{x}_n\}$ consisting of eigenvectors of $A$.
  2. If $\{\bf{x}_1,\dots,\bf{x}_n\}$ is a basis of eigenvectors of $A$ and $\lambda_{i}$ is the eigenvalue of $A$ corresponding to $\bf{x}_i$ for each $i$ (that is, $A\bf{x}_i=\lambda_i \bf{x}_i$), then $P^{-1}AP=\operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n)$.

Given an $n\times n$ matrix $A$, recall that to determine if it is diagonalizable (and find a diagonalizing matrix) we had to:

  1. Compute the eigenvalues of $A$ (that is, the roots of $c_A(x)$).
  2. For each eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $A$, find a set of basic eigenvectors (that is, a set of basic solutions to the matrix equation $(\lambda I - A)\bf{x}=\bf{0}$).
  3. Check if altogether we obtain $n$ basic eigenvectors for $A$.
This procedure can now be reinterpreted using subspaces and bases.

If $A$ is a square matrix and $\lambda \in \RR$ is a real number, the eigenspace of $A$ corresponding to $\lambda$ is \[ E_\lambda(A)=\{ \bf{x}\in\RR^n \mid A\bf{x}=\lambda \bf{x} \} = \null(\lambda I - A). \] Observe that $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue if and only if $E_{\lambda}(A)\neq \{ \bf{0} \} $.

With this nomenclature, we have that a set of basic eigenvectors for $A$ associated with an eigenvalue $\lambda$ is exactly the same as a basis for $E_\lambda(A)$.

Let $A$ be an $n\times n$ matrix and $\lambda\in \RR$ an eigenvalue of $A$. If $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of multiplicity $n$, then \[ \dim E_{\lambda}(A)\leq m. \]
Proof.

Let us set $\dim E_{\lambda}(A)=k$, and suppose $\{\bf{x}_1,\dots,\bf{x}_k\}$ is a basis of $E_{\lambda}(A)$. Then we may expand $\{\bf{1},\dots,\bf{x}_k\}$ to a basis $\{\bf{x}_1,\dots,\bf{x}_k,\bf{x}_{k+1},\dots,\bf{x}_n\}$ of $\RR^n$. Consider the matrix \[ P = \begin{bmatrix} \bf{x}_1 & \cdots & \bf{x}_k & \bf{x}_{k+1} & \cdots & \bf{x}_n \end{bmatrix}. \] Then $P$ is an invertible matrix because its columns are a basis of $\RR^n$. We compute $P^{-1}AP$: \[ \begin{aligned} P^{-1}AP &= P^{-1}A \begin{bmatrix} \bf{x}_1 & \cdots & \bf{x}_k & \bf{x}_{k+1} & \cdots & \bf{x}_n \end{bmatrix} = P^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1\bf{x}_1 & \cdots & \lambda_k\bf{x}_k & A\bf{x}_{k+1} & \cdots & A\bf{x}_n \end{bmatrix}\\[1em] &= \begin{bmatrix} \lambda \bf{e}_1 & \cdots & \lambda \bf{e}_k & P^{-1}A\bf{x}_{k+1} & \cdots & P^{-1}A\bf{x}_n \end{bmatrix}\\[1em] &= \begin{bmatrix} \lambda I_k & X \\ 0 & Y \end{bmatrix}, \end{aligned} \] where the last matrix is written in block form and $X$ and $Y$ are unknown matrices.

Since $A$ and $P^{-1}AP$ are similar, they have the same characteristic polynomial, so that \[ c_{A}(x) = \det \begin{bmatrix} (x-\lambda)I_k & X \\ 0 & xI -Y \end{bmatrix} = (x-\lambda)^{k}c_{Y}(x). \] This implies that $(x-\lambda)^{k}$ divides $c_A(x)$, so that $\dim E_{\lambda}(A)=k\leq m$, as desired.

Let $A$ be an $n\times n$ matrix. If $\bf{x}_1,\,\dots,\,\bf{x}_k$ are nonzero eigenvectors of $A$ whose corresponding eigenvalues $\lambda_1,\,\dots,\,\lambda_k$ are all distinct, then $\{\bf{x}_1,\dots,\bf{x}_k\}$ is a linearly independent set.
Proof.

We show that the result is true for $k=1,2,3$, and the general case can be done similarly.

For $k=1$, the result follows from the fact that a set formed by a nonzero vector is linearly independent.

Let us consider the case $k=2$, so that we have two nonzero eigenvectors $\bf{x}_1,\,\bf{x}_2\in \RR^n$ corresponding to different eigenvalues $\lambda_1,\,\lambda_2\in \RR$. If $t_1\bf{x}_1+t_2\bf{x}_2=\bf{0}$ for some scalars $t_1,\,t_2$, we can multiply this equation by $A$ and by $\lambda_1$ separately to obtain \[ t_1\lambda_1\bf{x}_1+t_2\lambda_2\bf{x}_2 = \bf{0},\qquad t_1\lambda_1\bf{x}_1+t_2\lambda_1\bf{x}_2 = \bf{0}. \] Subtracting both equations gives \[ t_2(\lambda_2-\lambda_1)\bf{x}_2 = \bf{0}, \] and since both $\lambda_2-\lambda_1$ and $\bf{x}_2$ are nonzero we conclude that $t_2=0$. Consequently, $t_1\bf{x}_1=\bf{0}$, so $t_1=0$ as well. Therefore, $\{\bf{x}_1,\bf{x}_2\}$ is linearly independent.

Now we do the case $k=3$. Suppose $\bf{x}_1,\,\bf{x}_2,\,\bf{x}_3\in \RR^n$ are nonzero eigenvectors of $A$ corresponding to different eigenvalues $\lambda_1,\,\lambda_2,\,\lambda_3\in \RR$. We have to prove that these vectors are linearly independent.

Consider scalars $t_1,\,t_2,\,t_3\in \RR$ satisfying \[ t_1\bf{x}_1+t_2\bf{x}_2 + t_3 \bf{x}_3 = \bf{0}. \] On one hand, multiplying the above equation by $A$, we obtain \[ t_1\lambda_1\bf{x}_1+t_2\lambda_2\bf{x}_2+t_3\lambda_3\bf{x}_3 = \bf{0}. \] On the other hand, multiplying by $\lambda_1$ instead we get \[ t_1\lambda_1\bf{x}_1+t_2\lambda_1\bf{x}_2+t_3\lambda_1\bf{x}_3 = \bf{0}. \] Subtracting both equations yields \[ t_2(\lambda_2-\lambda_1)\bf{x}_2 + t_3(\lambda_3-\lambda_1)\bf{x}_3 = \bf{0}. \] Now, $\bf{x}_2$ and $\bf{x}_3$ are linearly independent from the case $k=2$, so we must have \[ t_2(\lambda_2-\lambda_1)=t_3(\lambda_3-\lambda_1)=0, \] and since the eigenvalues under consideration are all distinct, we must have $t_2=t_3=0$. This yields $t_1\bf{x}_1=\bf{0}$, so that $t_1=0$, and we deduce that $\{\bf{x}_1,\bf{x}_2,\bf{x}_3\}$ is a linearly independent set.

If $A$ is an $n\times n$ matrix that has $n$ distinct eigenvalues, then $A$ is diagonalizable.
Proof.

Let $\lambda_1,\,\dots,\,\lambda_n$ be the distinct eigenvalues of $A$, and choose a nonzero eigenvector $\bf{x}_i$ for each $\lambda_i$. Then $\{\bf{x}_1,\dots,\bf{x}_n\}$ is a linearly independent set by the preceding theorem, and since $\dim \RR^n = n$, it follows that this set is a basis of $\RR^n$ as well. Thus, $A$ is diagonalizable because it admits a basis formed by eigenvectors.

From the preceding results, it follows that in order for an $n\times n$ matrix $A$ to be diagonalizable, we need two things to happen:

  • The characteristic polynomial $c_A(x)$ has to have only real roots (because the characteristic polynomial of a diagonal matrix only has real roots).
  • For each eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $A$, the dimension of $E_{\lambda}(A)$ has to be the same as the multiplicity of $\lambda$.

The converse is also true: suppose the above conditions are satisfied and let $\lambda_1,\,\dots,\,\lambda_{k}$ be the eigenvalues of $A$. If we choose bases $B_1,\,\dots,\,B_k$ for each of the eigenspaces $E_{\lambda_1}(A),\,\dots,\,E_{\lambda_k}(A)$, then from the fact that eigenvectors coming from different eigenvalues are linearly independent it follows that the union of the sets $B_1,\,\dots,\,B_k$ is a linearly independent subset $B$ of $\RR^n$. Now, we are assuming that \[ c_A(x)=(x-\lambda_1)^{m_1}\cdots(x-\lambda_k)^{m_k}, \] where the number $m_i$ is the multiplicity of $\lambda_i$ for each $i$, and (again, by assumption), $\dim E_{\lambda_i}=m_i$ for each $i$. Therefore, the number of elements of $B$ is \[ \dim E_{\lambda_1}(A) +\cdots+ \dim E_{\lambda_k}(A) = m_1 + \cdots + m_k = n, \] so $B$ is necessarily a basis of $\RR^n$ consisting of eigenvectors of $A$.

This discussion is summarized by means of the diagonal criterion:

An $n\times n$ matrix $A$ is diagonalizable if and only if
  1. The characteristic polynomial $c_A(x)$ only has real roots.
  2. For each eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $A$, the dimension of $E_{\lambda}(A)$ is equal to the multiplicity of $\lambda$.

We remark that if $c_A(x)$ has complex roots (that are not real), one could wonder if $A$ is diagonalizable over the complex numbers. More precisely, we say that $A$ is diagonalizable over the complex numbers if there exists a diagonal matrix $D$ with complex coefficients and an invertible matrix $P$ with complex coefficients such that $D=P^{-1}AP$. The methods for diagonalizing matrices over the complex numbers work in exactly the same way as the ones that we have discussed for real numbers. However, this goes beyond the scope of this course.

Decide if the matrix \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \] is diagonalizable, and find a diagonalizing matrix if so.
Solution.

The characteristic polynomial $c_A(x)$ of $A$ is \[ \begin{aligned} c_A(x) &= \left\lvert \begin{array}{ccc} x-1 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & x-2 & -1 \\ 0 &0 & x-1 \end{array} \right\rvert =(x-1)^{2}(x-2), \end{aligned} \] and thus we have two eigenvalues for $A$ (all of which are real): $\lambda_1=1$ and $\lambda_2=2$.

Let us find a basis and the dimension of $E_{1}(A)$. We have \[ E_{1}(A) = \null(I-A) = \null \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & -1 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}. \] Thus, a basis of $E_{1}(A)$ is \[ B_1 = \{ (1,-1,0),(0,1,-1) \} , \] which shows that $\dim E_{1}(A)=2$ is equal to the multiplicity of $1$.

We now deal with the eigenspace $E_{2}(A)$, which is \[ E_{2}(A) = \null(2I-A) = \null \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \] One sees using Gaussian elimination that \[ B_{2}=\{(0,1,0)\} \] is a basis of $E_{2}(A)$, so $\dim E_{2}(A)=1$ coincides with the multiplicity of $2$.

Now, because of the diagonal criterion, we can assert already that $A$ is diagonalizable. Moreover, \[ B=\{(1,-1,0),(0,1,-1),(0,1,0)\} \] is a basis of $\RR^3$ consisting of eigenvectors of $A$. Consequently, if we let \[ P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \] we obtain that $P$ is an invertible matrix satisfying that \[ P^{-1}AP = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \] is diagonal.
