3.2 Determinants and Matrix Inverses

In this section, we will discuss some further properties of determinants and their relationship with matrix products and inverses. More specifically, we will develop a test to check when a square matrix $A$ is invertible by means of its determinant.

We start with the following important result:

Suppose $A$ and $B$ are square matrices of the same size. Then, the determinant of the matrix product $AB$ is \[ \det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B). \]

While the formula for the determinant of $AB$ is seemingly simple, it is actually quite hard to prove that it is indeed correct. As one may expect, the formula above extends to products of more than two matrices. Indeed, if $A_1,\,\dots,\,A_k$ are square matrices of the same size, then we have \[ \det(A_1\cdots A_k) = \det(A_1) \cdots \det(A_k). \]

Now, suppose for a moment that we are given an invertible square matrix $A$. Then, the inverse $A^{-1}$ satisfies $AA^{-1}=I_n$. If we take determinants and use the fact that the determinant of a product is the product of the determinants of the factors, we get \[ 1=\det(I_n)=\det(AA^{-1})=\det(A)\det(A^{-1}). \] There are two things that we should read from this equation. The first one is that $\det(A)$ is nonzero, because when we multiply it by $\det(A^{-1})$ we obtain $1$ (a number different from zero). The second one is that we can solve for $\det(A^{-1})$ and obtain \[ \det(A^{-1})=\frac{1}{\det(A)}. \]

Remarkably, if $\det(A)\neq 0$ for some square matrix $A$, then one can show that $A$ is invertible. This yields the following result, known as the inverse criterion:

Suppose $A$ is a square matrix. Then the following statements hold:
  1. $A$ is invertible if and only if $\det(A)\neq 0$.
  2. If $A$ is invertible, the determinant of $A^{-1}$ is $\det(A^{-1})=\frac{1}{\det(A)}$.
Consider the matrix \[ A= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 7 & c+14 & 8 \\ 2 & 5 & c+3 \end{bmatrix}, \] where $c$ is a real number. For which values of $c$ is $A$ an invertible matrix?
Solution.

We compute $\det(A)$ by performing some column operations: \[ \begin{aligned} \left|\begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 7 & c+14 & 8 \\ 2 & 5 & c+3 \end{array}\right| \end{aligned} = \left|\begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 7 & c & 1 \\ 2 & 1 & c+1 \end{array}\right| = \left|\begin{array}{cc} c & 1 \\ 1 & c+1 \end{array}\right| = c^2+c-1. \] Therefore, $A$ is not invertible if $c^2+c-1=0$, which is the same as $c=\frac{-1 + \sqrt{5}}{2}$ or $c=\frac{-1 - \sqrt{5}}{2}$. This means that $A$ is invertible if and only if $c\neq\frac{-1 + \sqrt{5}}{2}$ and $c\neq\frac{-1 - \sqrt{5}}{2}$.

Suppose $A$ and $B$ are square matrices of the same size and $AB$ are invertible. Show that $A$ and $B$ are both invertible.
Solution.

Since $AB$ is an invertible matrix, the inverse criterion implies that $\det(AB)\neq 0$. Moreover, recall that $\det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B)$ because the determinant of a product is the product of the determinants of the factors, so $\det(A)\det(B)\neq 0$. This implies that both $\det(A)$ and $\det(B)$ are different from zero (otherwise, their product would be zero), so the inverse criterion implies that both $A$ and $B$ are invertible.

We finish this section by mentioning the relationship between determinants and transpose matrices.
If $A$ is any square matrix, then $\det(A)=\det(A^T)$.
If $\det(A)=-2$ and $\det(B)=3$, find $\det\big(B^2A^{-1}B^T(A^2)^T\big)$.
Solution. \[ \begin{aligned} \det\big(B^2A^{-1}B^T(A^2)^T\big) &= \det(B^2) \det(A^{-1}) \det(B^T) \det\big((A^2)^T\big) = \det(B)^2 \frac{1}{\det(A)} \det(B) \det(A^2) \\ &= \det(B)^2 \frac{1}{\det(A)} \det(B) \det(A)^2 = \det(B)^3 \det(A) \\ &= 3^3\cdot (-2) = -54. \end{aligned} \]
